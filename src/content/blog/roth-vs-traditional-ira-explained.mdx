Roth vs Traditional IRA is fundamentally a tax timing decision:

- **Roth IRA**: pay taxes now, potentially tax-free withdrawals later (if rules are met)
- **Traditional IRA**: potentially reduce taxes now, pay taxes on withdrawals later

To compare scenarios with your assumptions, use the [Roth vs Traditional IRA Calculator](/roth-vs-traditional-ira-calculator).

## The simplest decision framework

Many people start with this rule of thumb:

- If you expect your tax rate to be **higher later**, Roth can be attractive.
- If you expect your tax rate to be **lower later**, Traditional can be attractive.

But real life includes:

- contribution limits and eligibility rules
- employer retirement plans
- future income uncertainty

## Example: taxes now vs taxes later

If you contribute $6,500:

- Traditional might reduce taxable income today (if deductible)
- Roth does not reduce taxes today, but qualified withdrawals can be tax-free

## How growth changes the decision

The more growth you expect over decades, the more valuable Roth’s tax-free withdrawals can be—assuming your tax rate later is not dramatically lower.

## FAQ

### Is Roth always better for young people?
Not always, but younger savers often have lower current tax rates and longer time horizons, which can make Roth appealing.

### Can I do both Roth and Traditional?
In many cases, yes (subject to limits and eligibility). Some people diversify tax exposure by contributing to both.

### Are Traditional IRA contributions always deductible?
Not always. Deductibility depends on income and whether you or your spouse have a workplace retirement plan.

### Are Roth withdrawals always tax-free?
Qualified withdrawals can be tax-free, but there are rules (including holding period and age requirements).
