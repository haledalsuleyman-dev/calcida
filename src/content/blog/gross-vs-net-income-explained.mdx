When you receive a job offer for $70,000 a year, that number is your **gross income**. However, the amount that actually hits your bank account every month—your **net income**—will be significantly lower.

## What is Gross Income?

Gross income is the total amount of money you earn before any taxes or other deductions are taken out. For most employees, this is the base salary or hourly wage agreed upon with the employer.

## What is Net Income?

Net income, also known as \"take-home pay,\" is what remains after all mandatory and voluntary deductions have been subtracted from your gross pay.

## Common Paycheck Deductions

Several factors bridge the gap between gross and net income:

1.  **Federal Income Tax**: A progressive tax based on your income level.
2.  **FICA Taxes**: Mandatory contributions for Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%).
3.  **State and Local Taxes**: Depending on where you live, these can range from 0% to over 10%.
4.  **Retirement Contributions**: Pre-tax contributions to a 401(k) or 403(b).
5.  **Health Insurance Premiums**: Your share of medical, dental, and vision insurance costs.

## Why Does It Matter?

Understanding your net income is essential for creating a realistic budget. If you base your expenses (like rent or car payments) on your gross income, you will likely find yourself overextended.

## Summary

- **Gross Income**: Total earnings before deductions.
- **Net Income**: Actual pay received after taxes and benefits.

Always use your net income when planning your monthly budget to ensure financial stability.
