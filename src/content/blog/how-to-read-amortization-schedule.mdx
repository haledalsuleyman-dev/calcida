## The Most Depressing Document in Finance?

When you close on a house, you get a stack of papers. Buried in there is the **Amortization Schedule**. Most people glance at it and ignore it.

But if you look closely, it can be shocking. On a new 30-year loan, you might send the bank a check for $2,000, and see your loan balance drop by only $300. Where did the other $1,700 go? **Interest.**

## What is Amortization?

Amortization is the process of spreading out a loan into a series of fixed payments over time. While your total payment stays the same, the *composition* of that payment changes every single month.

### The Formula Logic
Interest is calculated on your *current* outstanding balance.
1.  **Month 1:** Your balance is huge (e.g., $400,000). Interest is calculated on $400k. That interest is paid first. The leftovers go to principal.
2.  **Month 120 (Year 10):** Your balance is lower. Interest is calculated on that lower number. Less interest is owed, so *more* of your fixed payment goes to principal.
3.  **Month 350:** Balance is tiny. Almost no interest accrues. Nearly the entire payment goes to principal.

## How to Read the Table

An amortization schedule typically has columns for:
*   **Payment Date**
*   **Payment Amount** (Fixed)
*   **Interest Paid** (Decreases over time)
*   **Principal Paid** (Increases over time)
*   **Remaining Balance**

### The "Tipping Point"
On a 30-year loan, you usually don't reach the "tipping point"—where you pay more principal than interest in a month—until roughly **year 18 or 19**.

## Why It Matters

Understanding amortization is the key to wealth building.

1.  **Motivation:** Seeing that only $200 of your payment is going to principal might make you angry enough to add an extra $50/month. That $50 goes 100% to principal, effectively boosting your "equity payment" by 25%!
2.  **Refinance Decisions:** If you are at year 20 of a mortgage, you are paying mostly principal. Refinancing to a new 30-year loan throws you back to year 1, where payments are mostly interest.
3.  **Selling:** It explains why, after owning a home for 5 years, you might not have as much equity as you thought (assuming home prices stayed flat).

## Summary

Don't ignore the amortization schedule. Print it out. Circle the date where you reach the tipping point. Then, use extra payments to move that date forward.
